This paper is the ﬁrst attempt to apply improved-physics-informed neural networks (I-PINNs) to 
simulate ﬂuid ﬂow in fractured porous media based on the reinterpreted discrete fracture model 
(RDFM). The RDFM, ﬁrst introduced by Xu and Yang, is a hybrid-dimensional model where Dirac-
delta functions are used to characterize fractures and superposed with the permeability tensor. In 
this paper, we apply the physical information neural networks (PINNs) to RDFM. Diﬀerent from 
the traditional PINNs where the PDE residual was used as the loss function, we adopt the ﬁnite 
element discretization of RDFM to build the loss function, avoiding the large gradient problem 
and diﬃculties in automatic diﬀerentiation. This new method is named as the improved PINNs 
(I-PINNs). Moreover, we combine the RDFM with incompressible miscible displacement in porous 
media. The bound-preserving technique of the I-PINNs is proposed and applied to the coupled 
system mentioned above, keeping the numerical concentration to be between 0 and 1. It is worth 
noting that one of the advantages of I-PINNs compared to PINNs is that it can better capture 
the pressure gradient at the fractures. Compared with traditional ﬁnite element methods for ﬂow 
equations, I-PINNs do not request the inversion of the stiﬀness matrix. In addition, diﬀerent from 
the traditional bound-preserving technique for contaminant transportation, I-PINNs preserve the 
physical bounds without taking a limited time step. Several numerical experiments are given to 
verify the feasibility and accuracy of the I-PINNs.
1. Introduction
In recent years, the study of ﬂuid ﬂow in fractured porous media has attracted a lot of attention due to the popularity of hydraulic 
fracturing technology in unconventional reservoir development. However, a variety of factors such as the vast diﬀerence in perme-
ability between fractures and matrix, the complexity of the fracture network’s geometric shapes, among others, present signiﬁcant 
diﬃculties and challenges in modeling and simulating the problem. Hence, it is essential to accurately establish a mathematical model 
of ﬂuid ﬂow in fractured porous media as well as an eﬃcient numerical method.

For these reasons, in recent decades researchers have developed a number of models depending on the treatment of the fractures, 
which can be categorized as continuum models and discrete fracture-matrix models. The dual porosity model [1–4]i s a typical 
continuum media model, which treats the matrix and fracture network as a continuous system with diﬀerent permeabilities, governed 
by Darcy’s Law. However, it generates certain errors when simulating disconnected fractured media. Due to the aforementioned 
reasons, researchers have developed a range of discrete fracture-matrix models to accurately describe individual fracture, including: 
the discrete fracture model (DFM)[5–10], embedded discrete fracture model (EDFM)[11–14], extended ﬁnite element DFM (XFEM-
DFM)[15–17], and so on.
The main idea of the DFM is to view the fractures as s-1 dimensional entries on the interface of s-dimensional matrix cells, 
with the thickness of fractures being the dimensional homogeneity factor. Since the fractures must be located at the edges of the 
matrix elements, it is diﬃcult to generate high-quality meshes when the fracture network is very complex. Therefore, embedded 
DFM (EDFM) has emerged as an alternative solution. It successfully avoids the mesh limitation and is widely used in real reservoir 
problems. Another class of discrete fracture-matrix models is XFEM-DFM. It imposes interface conditions across fractures by enriching 
the function space in the elements that intersect the fractures.
In 2020, Xu and Yang [18] proposed a reinterpreted discrete fracture model (RDFM), which uses the Dirac-𝛿 function to char-
acterize the permeability tensor of fractures and provides a new hybrid-dimensional representation of the permeability tensor. Its 
advantage lies in avoiding the mesh restrictions imposed by traditional DFM. Additionally, fractures and matrix are coupled in one 
system, and the computational cost does not increase with the increasing number of fractures. Building on this work, Guo etal.[ 19]
proposed conservative numerical methods for the RDFM and extended them to the contaminant transportation. Xu et al. [20,21]
and Fu etal.[ 22,23]used  RDFM to simulate ﬂow in porous media containing both fractures and barriers. After that, Wu etal.[ 24]
extended this novel model to Darcy-Forchheimer ﬂow in fractured porous media.
With the explosive growth of computing resources in the past decade, machine learning methods have undergone revolutionary 
development, such as the deep neural networks (DNNs)[25–27], the convolutional neural networks (CNNs)[28–30]and  the recurrent 
neural networks (RNNs)[31–33], etc. In recent years, anovel  DNN framework has been developed, called Physics-informed neural 
networks (PINNs) [34]. This approach was ﬁrst proposed to solve the forward and inverse problems described by the combination 
of some data and partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs) [35]. It has been utilized to various ﬂuid mechanics[36], elasticity[37]and  
seepage mechanics problems[38]. Some attractive features of the PINNs are summarized as follows: (a.)Compared  with traditional 
numerical methods, PINN belongs to the category of meshless methods, so it can easily deal with complex mobile domain problems; 
(b.) The employment of the PINNs provides closed-form and diﬀerentiable solutions which can be easily used in any subsequent 
calculation; (c.)Based  on embedded physical laws (basic PDE), PINN can be trained with a small amount of measured data; (d.)The  
code is easy to implement by using existing open-source deep learning algorithms.
Though PINN is a powerful tool in solving PDEs, it cannot yield accurate numerical approximations for RDFM, and we will use 
numerical experiments to demonstrate this. In this paper, we propose the improved-physics-informed neural networks (I-PINNs) 
framework suitable for RDFM and extend them to contaminant transportation. We have made some signiﬁcant breakthroughs in 
the above work: (a.) The I-PINNs framework proposed in this paper for solving single-phase ﬂow in fractured porous media takes 
the coordinates of collection points as input and the pressure ﬁeld as output, establishing a map from coordinates to pressure and 
possessing good surrogate model characteristics [38]. (b.) When calculating the single-phase ﬂow problem in porous media with 
fractures, the PINNs’ automatic diﬀerentiation technique is inevitably needed to calculate the pressure gradient at the fractures, and 
such technique may fail to work[39]. In the I-PINNs’ framework, the ﬁnite element discretization was used to represent the pressure 
gradient, eliminating the diﬃculties in automatic diﬀerentiation. (c.) We use a ﬁnite element discretization scheme to construct an 
approximation of the PDE residual loss function, avoiding the penalty terms of adjusting the loss from the boundary loss and the 
control equation in the traditional PINNs, and improving the eﬃciency [40]. (d.) Physically, the concentration in the contaminant 
transportation equation is between 0 and 1. Therefore, we propose bound-preserving I-PINNs in our paper. Compared with the 
traditional bound-preserving techniques, e.g. [41], our technique does not require a limited the time step size 𝑑𝑡, which greatly 
increases the computational eﬃciency and reduces the computational cost [42].
The rest of this article is as follows. Section 2 reviews the RDFM for single-phase ﬂow in fractured porous media. Section 3
describes the I-PINNs framework and applies it to RDFM. In Section 4, we use several numerical experiments to demonstrate the 
eﬀectiveness and accuracy of the proposed technique. The application in contaminant transportation is shown in Section5. Finally, 
we make some concluding statements in Section6.

3.4. Improved-physics-informed neural networks (I-PINNs)
Based on the idea of PINNs, we develop the I-PINNs framework to simulate the RDFM. In conventional PINNs, the outputs are 
substituted into the control equations and the residuals are computed by automatic diﬀerentiation (AD). AD is commonly used to 
train neural networks by calculating the derivative of a function at a point, rather than by analytically deriving an expression for the 
derivative of the function. We know that high gradients pose a serious challenge to the accuracy of AD, and it has not yet been shown 
in the literature that the continuity of ﬂuxes between mesh cells of diﬀerent properties can be accurately portrayed using AD.
In I-PINNs, we ﬁrst discretize the governing equations (2.8)by  FEM. Then the loss function is evaluated based on FEM. By doing 
so, the PDE residuals in PINNs are approximated by substituting the output of the neural network into the numerical schemes instead 
of the original PDEs, which avoids automatic diﬀerentiation and thus improves the accuracy.

6. Conclusion
In this paper, we proposed the novel I-PINNs framework and applied it to RDFM for single-phase ﬂow. Meanwhile, we proposed a 
boundary-preserving technique based on I-PINNs and successfully applied it to simulate the contaminant transportation in fractured 
porous media. Compared with the traditional PINNs, I-PINNs could get rid of the large errors in the computation of the extreme 


--- PAGE 18 ---
Journal of Computational Physics 520 (2025) 113491
18
C .W a n g ,H .G u o ,X .Y a ne ta l .
heterogeneous problems using the automatic diﬀerentiation technique, and at the same time, avoided the process of matrix inversion 
compared to the traditional numerical methods. Additionally, it had good agent modeling characteristics.
The examples presented in this paper demonstrated that the method based on I-PINNs was able to solve the fracture problem 
eﬃciently and with very high accuracy. It was also important to emphasize that the I-PINNs-based approach could easily be used 
to deal with complex fracture networks and 3D fracture structures since the variety of training parameters could be kept almost 
constant as the number and dimension of the fractures increased. In order to gain insight into the model, we also analyzed and 
quantiﬁed the performance of the I-PINNs model by selecting diﬀerent underlying network architectures. Nevertheless, I-PINNs still 
have some limitations in modeling fractured porous media problems. For example, it was not computationally eﬃcient when dealing 
with multiple tiny fractures. Currently, we have incorporated GPU parallelism into our approach to solve this problem. In conclusion, 
the developed I-PINNs framework could be considered as a powerful alternative to the single-phase ﬂow problem in fractured porous 
media.
